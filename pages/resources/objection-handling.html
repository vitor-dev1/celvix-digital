Handle Any Objection With Confidence

An objection is not a "no." It's a request for more certainty — and certainty is exactly what a great agent provides.

Deals stall when agents take objections personally or answer them defensively. The professionals stay calm, because they've heard every objection before and have a tested response ready. This guide gives you a reframing framework plus word-for-word scripts for the objections you'll hear most from both sellers and buyers.

The mindset: objections mean interest

People who aren't interested don't object — they disappear. An objection means the prospect is engaged enough to voice a concern. Your job isn't to "overcome" them like an opponent; it's to understand the real worry underneath and resolve it. Lead with curiosity, not rebuttal.

The reframing framework (use this every time)

  1. Acknowledge: Validate the concern so they feel heard. "That's a fair question — a lot of my clients think about that."
  2. Clarify: Ask a question to find the real issue. "When you say it feels high, are you comparing it to a specific home?"
  3. Reframe: Offer perspective or evidence that shifts the frame. Bring data, comps, or a different angle.
  4. Confirm: Check that you've resolved it and move forward. "Does that put your mind at ease? Great — let's talk next steps."

Common seller objections — and responses

"Another agent said they'd list it higher."

"I understand — a higher number is tempting. But the agent who quotes the highest price isn't the one who gets you the most money; the market does that. Overpricing usually means more days on market and a lower final sale. Here's what the comparable sales actually support, and here's the strategy to get you top dollar."

"Your commission is too high."

"I hear you, and it's right to ask what you're paying for. My marketing, pricing, and negotiation are built to net you more — even after fees — than a cheaper, hands-off approach. Let me show you exactly what I do that a discount option doesn't."

"We want to wait for the market to improve."

"That's a reasonable instinct. Let's look at where inventory, rates, and buyer demand are actually heading — waiting can cost more than it saves. Here's the data so we decide on facts, not fear."

Common buyer objections — and responses

"We want to think about it."

"Totally fair — it's a big decision. Just so I can help: is it the home itself, the price, or the timing you'd like to think through?" (Then address the real concern with the framework above.)

"The price is too high."

"Let's pressure-test that together. Based on recent sales of similar homes, here's what the market supports. If we love this home, the question isn't just price — it's whether it's worth it to you and how we structure a smart offer."

"We're waiting for rates to drop."

"Understandable. Keep in mind that when rates drop, buyers flood back and prices and competition rise — you can refinance a rate, but you can't renegotiate the price you locked in. Let's run the numbers both ways."

Pro Tip

Rehearse your top five objections out loud until the responses are automatic. Under pressure you don't rise to the occasion — you fall to your level of preparation. Negotiation basics every agent should own

  • Anchor thoughtfully — the first credible number shapes the whole negotiation.
  • Negotiate on terms, not just price — close date, contingencies, repairs, and rent-backs all carry value.
  • Stay calm and silent — after you make an offer, let the other side respond first.
  • Keep emotion out — represent your client's interests, not your own ego.
  • Always know your client's walk-away point before you sit down.

Your objection-handling checklist

  • Treat every objection as a buying signal, not a rejection.
  • Acknowledge → Clarify → Reframe → Confirm, every time.
  • Ask a question before you answer — find the real concern.
  • Back your reframe with data, comps, or a clear example.
  • Rehearse your top five until they're second nature.
